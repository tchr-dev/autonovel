# Voice Trials — Ashen Crown

Same scene three ways: Rowan, sword-apprentice, kneels in the snow at the
edge of an old battlefield grave and lifts a broken crown out of frozen
mud. He does not yet know what it is.

---

## Trial A — Spare and cold (Anglo-Saxon, knife-cut sentences)

The grave was shallow because the ground had been frozen when they dug it.
Rowan saw the bone first. Then the iron.

He brushed the snow away. His glove tore on something hard.

A circle of black metal, broken at one side. Cold. Heavier than it looked.
He turned it. Old smith-marks, no jewels. Three points snapped off at the
roots. The fourth was the shape of a tooth.

His breath went white. The wind came down from the pass and stopped.

He thought: *crown.* He thought: *no.* Then he did not think anything.
The iron in his hand was warm.

---

## Trial B — Mythic and weighty (Latinate, bell-tone, long clauses)

Three hundred winters had fallen on Vaelgard since the last dragon-lord
was buried, and three hundred winters had laid their patient hands upon
this grave, and yet the snow that Rowan brushed aside seemed to him no
older than the morning. The earth gave grudgingly. Beneath his glove
he felt first the long and yellowed bone of a man who had been a king,
or a king's brother, or perhaps a thief who died wearing a king's
coronet — for the iron that came up after the bone was a circle, broken,
unjewelled, marked with the smith-runes of an age before the Sundering,
and Rowan, who was an apprentice and the son of nobody, knew its shape
at once because every child of Vaelgard knew its shape.

The wind that came down from the pass took the words from his throat
before he could shape them. The crown in his hand was warm.

---

## Trial C — Hybrid: cold-short for the present, old-tongue when memory speaks

The grave was shallow. Frozen ground refuses the shovel.

Rowan brushed the snow away with the back of his glove. Bone first, then
iron. A black ring, broken, no jewels, three points missing at the roots.
He turned it in his hand.

It was warm.

*Three hundred winters,* he thought, and the thought was not his —
it arrived already finished, in a voice that knew the shape of those
winters from the inside, that had counted them by the cold and the
red banners and the slow patient memory of stone. *The last of them
was buried in a shallow grave because the ground was frozen and the
men who dug it had been afraid.*

He let go. The crown stayed in his hand. The wind came down from the
pass and stopped at the edge of the grave as if it knew not to enter.

Then the thought left him, and he was a sword-apprentice again, and
his glove was torn, and he was holding a broken piece of iron that
should not have been warm.

---

# Evaluation

## Trial A — spare and cold
- **Achieves:** Immediacy. The body is in the snow. The reader feels the
  grave through Rowan's hands, not through the narrator's lecture. Punches
  hard for violence and discovery scenes.
- **Costs:** Hard to do interior weight. Hard to handle the dragon's
  true-name magic — the magic mechanic is *cosmic*, and the voice has
  no register for cosmic. Risks Cormac McCarthy pastiche.
- **Implies:** A novel about a man and his weapon. Closer to *The Last
  Kingdom* than to *Earthsea*.
- **Audience:** Readers who like Joe Abercrombie, Mark Lawrence.
- **AI tells:** Low — but watch for sentence-length uniformity in the
  short-clipped mode (the Tier 1 structural slop "every sentence 15-25
  words" trap).

## Trial B — mythic and weighty
- **Achieves:** Scale. Three hundred winters lands. The reader feels the
  crown's age the moment it touches the page. Great for the dragon
  sequences.
- **Costs:** Suffocates the small. When Rowan and Mira argue over a fire
  about a dead horse, this voice will sound ridiculous. Also a *very*
  short walk to the Tier 1 banned-word zone — "tapestry," "realm,"
  "embark" all want to crawl in. Long-clause sentences statistically
  collect AI-tells.
- **Implies:** Tolkien / Le Guin tradition. A novel where prose is
  scripture.
- **Audience:** Earthsea / Silmarillion readers. Smaller, demanding.
- **AI tells:** High risk. The "long clause-heavy sentence" mode is
  exactly where Claude/GPT defaults live.

## Trial C — hybrid (cold present + old-tongue memory)
- **Achieves:** *Voice enacts mechanic.* When Rowan uses the crown's
  power, the prose itself remembers things he doesn't, in a register
  older than him. When the power releases, the voice contracts back
  to a frightened apprentice in the snow. The reader can *hear* the
  memory cost. Also: the contrast between modes prevents either mode
  from going stale.
- **Costs:** Hardest to control. If the old-tongue mode gets too long
  it becomes Trial B and starts collecting slop. If the cold-short
  mode gets too uniform it becomes Trial A and reads as pastiche.
  Demands real attention to the *transitions* between modes.
- **Implies:** A novel about possession and dispossession. The
  protagonist is borrowed. Closest comp: Susanna Clarke, *Piranesi*;
  R. F. Kuang, *Babel*'s footnote-voice intrusions; Le Guin's
  *Tehanu* shifting register when Therru speaks.
- **Audience:** Readers who liked the seed's specific premise — the
  memory cost — and want the prose to honour it.
- **AI tells:** Mid risk. The old-tongue mode is the danger zone;
  must be rationed. Hard rule: it only appears when memory-magic is
  actually *firing*, never as scene-setting flourish.

## Choice

**Trial C — the hybrid.** The seed's whole engine is that magic costs
memory and the magic *is* naming. A voice that is itself two voices —
one that lives in Rowan's body, one that arrives from somewhere older
when he draws on the crown — does work no single register can do. It
makes the cost audible. When the old-tongue mode goes silent for two
chapters, the reader will notice, and that silence will mean something.
The discipline is in keeping the old-tongue mode rare and specifically
triggered: only when the crown is active, only when a true-name
surfaces, never as decoration.
