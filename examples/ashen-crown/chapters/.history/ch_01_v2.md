# Ch 1: Frozen Ground

The quench came clean.

Rowan held the blade-blank above the trough for a count of three, watching the heat go from yellow to red to that particular black that meant the steel was ready, and then he plunged it. The water gulped once. Steam came up in a single white column and was gone. Brann, who had not looked up from the smaller anvil, said, "Good."

It was the first proper quench of the new working year. The smithy still smelled faintly of last night's fish. The shared coal of Coalday sat at the back of the main forge, ringed in slag, almost gone. Last night the village had walked it from house to house in the iron tongs, and the iron had rung at every doorstep when the next house took the coal, and Rowan had heard it from the smithy where he had been banking the fire down to its long sleep. This morning they were a parish of twelve houses lit from one fire, and he had done his master's first quench without scorching it.

"Good," Brann said again, and that was the whole of it.

Rowan laid the blank on the cooling rack. His hands were shaking — not enough to see, just enough that he knew. He turned them palm-up and looked at them as if they belonged to someone else. The burn-scar on the meat of his right thumb. The hammer-bent knuckle on the left thumb where, at twelve, he had learned the wrong grip.

He tried, while he was looking at them, to pull up the smell of his mother's hair. He did this sometimes the way other men checked for a coin in a pocket. A long time ago, after a wash-day, she had let him press his face into her hair while it dried and the hair had smelled of woodsmoke and the green soap she made from pine-ash. He could call up the woodsmoke. The pine-ash was thinner than it had been last winter. The hair itself — the way of it — was somewhere under the other two and would not come up.

Just time, he thought.

Brann put the small hammer down. The smithy door was open to the south to let yesterday's smoke clear. Beyond the door the village street was beaten earth and old snow, and at the far end of it, walking towards them, was a man in a king's grey cloak.

"Rowan," Brann said. "Stand up straight."

The reeve's horse stopped at the smithy door. The reeve did not dismount. He was a thin man, mid-forties, ink-stained at the cuff. He had two saddle-bags and a writ-case and he looked tired in the way men look who have been on the road since before Coalday.

"Brann's Forge," he said. It was not a question.

"Aye." Brann came to the door. He was wiping his hands with a rag the colour of old quench-water. "You're early."

"I'm late," the reeve said. "Three parishes since dawn."

He opened the writ-case and did not look at the writ. He had said what was on it before, evidently. "The king is buying scrap iron from the old fields. Twice the going rate by the weight. Old battlefield work especially — broken arms, broken belt-buckles, anything from before the grandfathers. He pays in silver on the road, not on credit."

Brann's face did the thing it did when he was working out whether to lie. "We're a smith's parish. We don't keep scrap. We melt it."

"He'll buy what's not yet melted."

Brann looked at the reeve. The reeve looked tired and a long way from anywhere he wanted to be. He had the writ in his hand and he was not reading it.

"Anything specific the king's after?" Brann asked, the way a man asks who already knows the answer is yes.

"Broken iron," the reeve said. "Ring-shaped."

He said it without inflection, the way a reeve who had been told to say it without inflection would say it. He did not look at Rowan. He looked at the cooling-rack and the new black blank and beyond it at the back wall of the smithy where the coal-bin was. Then he looked at the road south.

"There's a field two days off-road," Brann said. "Marrow Field. Border skirmish, three winters back. Nothing the parish wanted to rake over." He paused as if reading what had already been read. "I can send my apprentice with a barrow."

"By tonight."

"By tomorrow night," Brann said. "He walks."

The reeve thought about that and did not argue. He took a leather pouch from the writ-case and laid three silver coins on the smithy step without dismounting. "On account. Twice weight when he comes back. I'll be at Threehearth two days from now."

He turned the horse. The horse went down the village street the way it had come, picking its feet up over the half-frozen mud. The reeve did not look back. Rowan watched him go past the bowyer's boarded shop and past the baker's house with its red-brown smoke and out at the south end where the road bent and was gone.

Brann picked up the three coins from the step and weighed them in his hand without looking at them.

"You'll go now," he said.

"Aye."

"You'll take the barrow and the second hammer and the long knife."

"Aye."

Brann was looking at the road south, not at him. "Listen, boy. You'll know what you're for when something asks for you. Not when something gives you a job. Mind that on the road."

"The king is —?"

"Giving you a job. Aye." Brann turned back to the smithy. "Eat first. Eat enough for tomorrow as well. The barrow's by the south door."

It was not the kind of leaving that gave space for a farewell. Rowan ate cold porridge and salted pork standing up. He took the long knife and the second hammer and he wheeled the empty barrow out of the smithy and down the village street and at the south bend he looked back once. Brann was at the smithy door, watching him go. Brann raised the hammer-hand. Rowan raised his back. Then he was on the road.

It was still only midday.

---

The road south of Brann's Forge was old and not much used in winter. Snow lay on it in a thin even crust where the cart-tracks had been and in pillows on either side where the pines began. Rowan walked because the barrow was empty and the snow was not deep enough for skis and because, if he was honest, he wanted to be tired by sundown.

He came to the wayside cairn an hour before dark. It was a stack of stones a man's height on the eastern shoulder of the road, with a peg of old iron driven into its top, and on the peg a small banner, ribbon-thin, knotted at the four corners. The cloth was red and faded and stained at one edge with what would once have been blood. A refugee mourning. Somebody had put it there on a long road and gone on without it.

Marrow Field opened east of the cairn, a wide low ground between two ridges. The pines thinned. Snow lay patchy on hard tussock-grass. He counted twelve paces east as Brann had said and on the twelfth he stopped.

The grave was shallow because the ground had been frozen when they dug it. A long mound, no longer than a tall man, dusted over now with new snow. No marker. No coin-stone. He knelt by the foot of it and brushed at the snow with the back of his glove and the ground beneath was hard and crusted and no honest burial had been here.

He took the long knife out and used it as a digger. The first three layers came up easy: snow, then frozen leaf-mould, then the dark earth that had been turned a winter ago and not packed down. The cold came up from it into his knees.

Bone first.

It was a long bone, yellowed, with the marrow-end showing where some animal had been at it before the burial. It was not the bone of a man who had been buried with respect. He laid it on the snow beside him. There were others. He did not look at the others.

Then iron.

He brushed the earth away. The iron was a circle, broken at one side, lying flat. He levered around it with the knife to free it and got his bare fingers under it and pulled and the glove tore on something hard at the inner edge.

He held it up.

A black ring. The size to fit a man's head. Broken cleanly at one side, no rust, no jewels, no chasing. Three points snapped off at the roots, which were jagged. The fourth point was the shape of a tooth. The metal under the dirt was not iron — not exactly. It looked like iron and weighed too much for its size and the surface was the colour of unlit coal and the back of his hand, where the torn glove had gone through, was bleeding without him having felt the cut.

The crown was warm.

Three hundred winters, he thought, and the thought was not his — it arrived already finished, in a voice that knew the shape of those winters from the inside, that had counted them by the cold and the red banners and the slow patient memory of stone, and the last of them was buried in a shallow grave because the ground was frozen and the men who dug it had been afraid, and they had not done it well, and one of them had sat afterwards on a cairn-stone an hour east of here and tied a red cloth onto a peg of iron because he had nobody to bury and because something had to be marked, and the road remembered him.

Then the thought left him.

He was a sword-apprentice again, kneeling in old earth, and his glove was torn, and his hand was bleeding on the back where it should not be, and he was holding a broken piece of iron that should not have been warm. The wind that had been coming down off the north ridge had stopped. He could hear his own breath.

He did not put the crown in the barrow.

The barrow stood empty four paces away. The bones lay on the snow. There was other scrap in the grave — he could feel a buckle under the heel of his hand and what felt like the broken pommel of a sword — and Brann had told him to bring back what he could, and the barrow was empty.

He put the crown inside his coat.

He pulled his shirt down over it so the iron lay flat against his ribs. The warmth of it went through the linen and stopped there as if it had reached the place it had been going. He sat back on his heels in the snow and did not move for a long time.

When he moved again he picked up the bones one at a time and laid them gently back in the grave. He filled the grave in with the loose earth and patted the snow flat over it with his ungloved hand. He took the buckle and the broken pommel and a handful of cut nails and a length of corroded chain and put those in the barrow. He stood up.

The cut on the back of his hand had stopped bleeding without him noticing. He pulled the torn glove off and looked at his hand. There was no cut.

He put the glove back on.

The walk back to the cairn took longer than the walk out. The barrow was loud in the snow and the iron under his shirt was a steady small heat against his skin and the wind found new ways into his coat. At the cairn he stopped. The little red banner was still on its peg. He looked at it for a moment as if it had asked him a question, and then he turned south, and walked.

He did not sleep well that night. The barrow was upturned beside him for a windbreak and his cloak was over him and the iron was inside his shirt and against his skin and warm. He dreamed, and in the dream he stood in a room with no door, and the walls of the room were black glass, and somewhere above him a bell was ringing slowly and patiently and not for him.

He woke with the bell still in his ears, and the iron still warm, and the morning grey and far off and not yet anything.
