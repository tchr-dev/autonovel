# World Bible — Vaelgard

## Cosmology & History

The world is unnamed in-text — characters call it *the country* or
*the world* or *under the sky*. There are seven kingdoms; only
three appear on the page (Vaelgard, the Iron North, the Salt Coast
to the south). The other four are referenced only as origins of
trade goods and refugee names.

### Origin

In the oldest songs, the mountains were not made by gods but by
**dragons that lay down to sleep and never woke**. The valleys are
the gaps between their bodies. The rivers are the lines of their
breath, frozen and then thawed for the first time. This is treated
in-text as folk cosmology — old men say it; nobody argues with it;
nobody confirms it; the dragon, when it speaks, neither denies nor
affirms it. It might be true. It might be the kind of song a
defeated people sing about its monsters.

### The Calendar

Vaelgard counts in **winters**, not years. A winter is the period
between one snow-line crossing the high passes and the next; it
runs roughly twelve months but is not exact, and disputes over the
turn of a winter are settled by the king's reeves and resented for
generations. The story takes place in the **three-hundred-and-tenth
winter since the Burning of Caer Vael**.

### The Burning of Caer Vael (≈300 winters before the story)

The last war between humans and dragons. Dragons had bonded to the
Vaelgard royal line for nine generations under the **Ashen Oath** —
a binding sworn at the dragon-shrines that traded human memory for
dragon power. The bonded kings used dragons to expand the country,
to break the iron tribes of the north, to silence the salt-priests
of the south. The dragons grew tired of being weapons. The queen
at the time, **Maeri Aldren**, refused to release the binding. The
dragons broke the oath at the cost of all of them, and burned Caer
Vael, the old capital, to its foundations.

In the burning, every adult dragon died. Every dragon-bonded royal
died with them, because the oath ran both ways. The eggs — the few
that had not yet hatched — went missing. Most are believed to have
been buried with their mothers in the high crags, or cracked and
gone to dust. **The egg in this book is the last that survived
intact.** Nobody knows this at story start except the dragon
inside it.

The line of Aldren survived only because Maeri's youngest brother
had been kept un-bonded as a precaution. He took the throne in
the ash. The current king, **Aldren the Sixth**, is his great-
great-grandson.

### Religion

There is no priesthood. There are **shrines** — small stone
structures, usually one room, always built of black basalt, found
at high places along old roads. Each shrine is a memorial to a
dragon that died in the Burning. Most are tended by no one and
half-buried in pine duff. A few are still kept clean by old
soldiers and oath-keepers who walked from the war and never quite
came back. Caldus is one of these.

Common people don't pray to the dragons. They keep small habits —
they don't sleep with iron under their pillows; they don't burn
pine on the longest night; they bury the dead with one coin in
the mouth, the old payment for crossing a remembered place. None
of them remember why.

## Magic System

### Hard Rules (NEVER violate)

1. **Dragon magic is true-naming.** Dragons remember the original
   names of mountains, rivers, storms, bloodlines, and stones. To
   speak a thing's true name is to command that thing — to still a
   river, to call a storm down a pass, to wake a sleeping mountain.
   No human, alone, knows any true names. Only a dragon, or a human
   bonded to a dragon, can use them.

2. **Bonding requires the Ashen Oath**, sworn at a dragon-shrine,
   sealed by the dragon and the human laying their right hands
   together on the **oath-stone** (a black basalt slab built into
   every shrine). The bond persists until one party dies. If the
   dragon dies, the human dies within minutes. If the human dies,
   the dragon survives but is *never able to bond again* — the
   memory of that human is part of the dragon's name now.

3. **The cost is memory, paid by the human.** Each use of a true
   name takes one specific human memory. Not random — the *most-
   loved* memory the bonded human currently carries. Loved in the
   strict sense: the memory whose loss will hurt most. The land
   takes payment in love. The dragon does not choose. The land
   does. *The bonded human does not feel the memory go until they
   reach for it later and find nothing.*

4. **The crown is not a true name. The crown is a focus.** It is
   a circlet of black basalt-iron forged at the founding shrine,
   and it lets a bonded human channel a true name without burning
   their throat out. (Speaking a true name unfocused costs the
   human their voice for days, sometimes for life.) The crown in
   this book is broken — three of its four points are missing —
   so it can focus only the simplest names: *cold, wind, stone.*
   The fourth, the tooth-shaped point, is the one that channels
   *fire.*

5. **A dragon cannot be commanded by its true name.** It can be
   commanded by *bond*, which is consent given at the shrine. The
   crown does not override consent — it only amplifies what bond
   already permits. *This is the seed's central trick: the king
   thinks the crown enslaves the dragon. It does not. It only
   amplifies what the dragon agrees to. Take away the bond, and
   the crown is iron.*

6. **Dragon eggs hatch only on contact with a living bonded
   human.** No bonded human, no hatching. The crown alone is not
   enough. Someone has to swear the Ashen Oath to the egg,
   accepting the cost, before the dragon can be born. Rowan has
   not yet done this when he finds the crown. The moment he does,
   the cost begins.

7. **The bond can be attempted at distance only via *fragment-
   ritual* — and only by a human who already holds a piece of an
   oath-stone.** Aldren has, kept secret in his keep, a thumb-
   sized chip broken from the Cradle Shrine oath-stone in the
   Burning, smuggled out by Aldren's great-great-grandfather. The
   corrupted oath he was taught uses this fragment to attempt
   binding without the dragon being present. *It cannot succeed
   without the consent clause* — but the attempt itself burns the
   human who tries. A fragment-binding refused by the dragon
   recoils through the fragment and into the bearer. This is what
   kills Aldren in Ch 11. The fragment is destroyed in the recoil.

8. **The first bond — bootstrap.** The Ashen Oath was not the
   first bond. Three thousand winters ago, before Vaelgard, a
   single dragon offered itself to a human at what would later be
   called Cradle Shrine, with no oath, no stone, no crown — only
   consent. The dragon spoke its true name into the human's mouth
   and the human spoke it back. From that bond, the *form* of the
   Ashen Oath was built; the basalt of the dragon's resting place
   became the first oath-stone; the structure built around it
   became the first shrine. All later bonds are recursions of that
   first private moment, formalised against future kings who could
   not be trusted with such intimacy unwitnessed. *(This bootstrap
   is folk knowledge in Caldus's possession; he tells Rowan in
   Ch 6 as part of explaining the consent clause. The text is
   almost throwaway. Aldren has never heard it.)*

### Soft Rules (can bend for story)

- **Memory loss is precise but not surgical.** Rowan loses a
  *whole memory* — a face, a moment, a name. He doesn't lose
  *facts* (he still knows his mother existed; he can't remember
  her face). The hole has shape.
- **Bonded humans dream in old-tongue.** Once bonded, the human
  starts to dream in a register their waking mind doesn't speak.
  This is how the prose's old-tongue mode is justified
  diegetically — by the time the dragon hatches, Rowan's *mind*
  has been entered.
- **True names can be partially known.** A bonded human can know
  the *kind* of name a thing has (cold-name, river-name) before
  the specific. This lets the dragon teach.
- **Dragons can refuse to use a name they know.** Bond is consent,
  not slavery. The dragon in this book refuses several times.

## Geography

Vaelgard is a mountain kingdom shaped like a **broken hand**: the
palm is the central plateau (where the current capital, **Hold-on-
Iron**, sits), the four fingers are valleys cut south by glacial
rivers, and the thumb is the **Pass of Caer Vael** — the road that
climbs north to the burned old capital and beyond, into the Iron
North.

Travel times (Rowan's POV; he walks, occasionally rides):
- **Smith's village (Brann's Forge) → Hold-on-Iron:** 4 days walking.
- **Hold-on-Iron → the battlefield grave:** 2 days, off-road.
- **The grave → the Pass of Caer Vael:** 3 days, climbing.
- **The Pass → Caer Vael ruins:** 1 day, ridge-walking.
- **Caer Vael → the founding shrine (Cradle Shrine):** half a day,
  along the old basalt road.
- **Cradle Shrine → the Iron North border:** 2 days down the
  northern slope. Varkesh's army is camped here.

Locations the book uses (in rough order of appearance):
1. **Brann's Forge** — Rowan's village. A smithy and twelve houses.
2. **Marrow Field** — old skirmish-ground, recent border fight,
   *not* the Burning. The crown was buried here recently, by
   someone. (Plot: Caldus.)
3. **Hold-on-Iron** — current capital. Stone-and-timber. Aldren's
   keep. Mira meets Rowan here.
4. **The Pass of Caer Vael** — high road north. "Haunted" in the
   sense that the wind sounds wrong; nothing alive happens.
5. **Caer Vael ruins** — the burned old capital. Black glass slag
   where dragon-fire melted stone.
6. **Cradle Shrine** — the founding shrine. Where the first Ashen
   Oath was sworn. Built around the oath-stone.
7. **The Iron North camp** — Varkesh's army, twenty thousand men,
   waiting for the pass to clear.

## Factions & Politics

### Vaelgard (Aldren's faction)
- King Aldren the Sixth: dying. Wasting illness. Has months.
- A small council of three lords (Thane Reska, Thane Olen, Thane
  Brae). Competent and afraid.
- The royal guard: 400 men, half garrisoned at Hold-on-Iron, half
  watching the southern road.
- The smith-houses (Rowan's class): not nobility, not peasants.
  Hereditary craft-clan. Hold quiet political weight; the king's
  reeves don't tax them, in exchange for war-iron in season.

### The Iron North (Varkesh's faction)
- General Varkesh, commanding twenty thousand men. Not a king —
  the Iron North is a confederacy of clan-holds, and Varkesh
  speaks for all of them by acclamation, not by birth.
- War goal: take the Vaelgard plateau before any new dragon-rumor
  matures. He believes (correctly, by the rules of the world) that
  a new dragon means a new age of bonded kings, and the iron
  tribes were the targets of the last age. Personal grievance:
  his great-grandmother's clan was burned out by a Vaelgard
  dragon in the year before the Burning.

### The oath-keepers (Caldus's faction-of-one)
- Surviving veterans of the last war. Almost all dead now. Caldus
  is the last who can recite the Ashen Oath in its full form. He
  has spent forty winters tending Cradle Shrine and refusing to
  speak to anyone who asks why.

### The dragon (its own faction)
- Has been awake inside the egg for three hundred winters,
  conscious, waiting. Has its own goals: it wants to be unbonded,
  it wants the Ashen Oath ended, and it does not want to be born
  into a world where a human will use its name to break things.

## Bestiary

The world is **almost empty of monsters**, deliberately. The
dragons were the only fantastical creatures, and they are gone.
Wolves are wolves. Bears are bears. Horses are horses. The reader
should feel the absence: this is a world that lost its magic and
has been a slightly poorer place ever since.

The dragon, when it hatches: about the size of a large dog at
hatching, but its body does not seem to obey scale — its shadow is
wrong, its weight is wrong, sometimes it seems much larger and the
room hasn't changed. (Diegetic: it is partly in another time; it
has not yet committed entirely to this one.) It grows fast once
fed; by the climax it is the size of a small horse.

## Cultural Details

### Language
- One spoken language, **Vaelish**, in the south of the country.
- The Iron North speaks **Norn**, related but separate. Rowan does
  not understand it; Varkesh's parley scenes use a translator.
- The **old tongue** is not a language anyone speaks. It is the
  language true-names belong to. It can be heard but not spoken
  by humans, except in dreams.

### Customs
- **Bury the dead with one coin in the mouth.** Vestigial — the
  coin used to be the offering for safe crossing of a "remembered
  place," i.e., a place a dragon's true name had touched. Nobody
  alive knows this.
- **Don't burn pine on the longest night.** Vestigial — pine smoke
  reads, in dragon memory, as a summons. Nobody alive remembers
  this either.
- **Smiths salute each other by raising the hammer-hand.** Living
  custom; functional; class marker.
- **Red banners are mourning. White is celebration.** Inverted
  from default medieval-Europe convention — a small detail to keep
  the world from defaulting to *generic medieval*.

### Festival — Coalday (plot-relevant)

The one festival the book uses. **Coalday** falls on the first
hard-frost morning of late winter — variable, declared each year
by the eldest smith of each parish when the anvil rings true-cold
under the morning hammer. On Coalday, every smith in the parish
banks their forge to a single shared fire, and the village walks
in procession from house to house with one coal carried in iron
tongs. Each household receives the coal, lights its hearth from
it, and returns the tongs. The day ends with a meal of black bread
and salted fish and one cup of *shrub* per adult.

Plot consequence: the story opens **the morning after Coalday**.
Rowan's quench at the start of Ch 1 is the first proper quench of
the new working year. The reeve arrives later that same day —
Coalday is when the king's reeves traditionally take their
inventory of the parish, and the king's interest in *broken iron,
ring-shaped* falls on a day when every smith in the country is
working and every road is being walked by reeves. This is not
coincidence: Aldren timed the search to Coalday because anyone
holding old battlefield iron will, on the morning after, take it
out and inventory it for the reeve.

The text does not over-explain Coalday. It appears once in Ch 1
(the procession the night before, glimpsed in Rowan's memory; the
shared coal still in Brann's main forge; the smell of yesterday's
fish), once in Ch 2 (Brann remarks that Coalday is supposed to be
a *small* day; the reeve's arrival makes it a large one), and
once in Ch 12 (Rowan walking south, end of the book — *next
Coalday I will not be in a parish that has me*).

### Village rhythm — Brann's Forge

Twelve houses and the smithy. The smithy is the largest building.
Most of the village works in or for it — two charcoalers, three
ore-runners (who go to the foothills six days a fortnight to bring
back small ore), a bowyer (who left for the south last winter and
has not come back, his shop boarded), and a baker who is also the
village's only literate person and reads the king's writs aloud.

The week's shape: hammer-days (four days, dawn to dusk), market-day
(one day, traders come up from the next parish with cloth and salt
and gossip), rest-day (one day), and travel-day (one day, when ore
goes out and finished work goes south). The village does not have
a priest, a reeve in residence, or a tavern — only a wide hearth
in the smithy where men sit on rest-day and hammer-day evenings
and Brann pours *shrub* from a clay jar he refills himself.

The reeve in Ch 1 / Ch 2 is the *king's* reeve, not a parish
official. His arrival is unusual — parish reeves come on Coalday
once a winter and otherwise stay south.

In any chapter where Rowan is in a village (Ch 1, Ch 2, Ch 8 — the
farm village), the prose should ground in this rhythm: somebody is
working, somebody is at the wide hearth, somebody is on the road
between parishes. Empty villages are a worldbuilding bug.

### Food
- Mountain country: hard cheese, salted pork, oat porridge, a sour
  beer called **shrub**. Pine-needle tea on the road.
- The dragon eats meat, raw and fast. At hatchling size (Ch 7-8):
  one rabbit a day, plus a handful of salt at dawn (the salt is
  not nutrition — it is part of the old preparation; see MYSTERY).
  By Ch 9-10, after a growth spurt: a small deer or two rabbits a
  day. By Ch 11-12: a half-grown sheep daily — Varkesh's parley
  party brings two on the third day specifically as an offering,
  and the offering is accepted as diplomacy. Caldus and Mira hunt
  the early days; once at Cradle Shrine, the basalt road's old
  cairns mark long-abandoned game-runs that the dragon teaches
  Rowan to read.

### Clothing
- Wool and leather. Nobles distinguished by collar (oxblood-dyed).
  The royal house wears a **red-on-grey** combination — red collar,
  grey wool — that reads as *mourning a dragon*, though most do
  not know that.

## Internal Consistency Rules

(Things the evaluator must check.)

1. The crown only focuses *cold, wind, stone* until the missing
   tooth-shaped point is found and refit. It cannot focus *fire*
   in the first half of the book. If the prose has Rowan calling
   fire with the broken crown, that's a bug.

2. Memory cost is paid in *most-loved* memories first. The
   sequence of what Rowan loses must be a descending ladder of
   love: small first (a particular morning's smithwork with his
   master), then larger (a friend's laugh), eventually catastrophic
   (his mother's face). Track this in canon.

3. The dragon does not hatch until Rowan swears the Ashen Oath
   *at a shrine*, with the oath-stone, with the egg. Crown alone
   does not hatch the egg.

4. Caldus knows the full Ashen Oath. Aldren and his council do
   *not* — they know a corrupted version that does not include
   the consent clause. (This is the political crux: when Aldren
   tries to use the dragon, he attempts to bind it without
   consent, and the binding fails. He blames Rowan.)

5. Varkesh has twenty thousand men. The Vaelgard royal guard has
   four hundred. Even with a hatched dragon, the numbers don't
   simply make this a battle Vaelgard wins. The dragon refuses to
   be a one-sided weapon. The ending is a *negotiation* under
   threat of mutual ruin, not a victory.

6. The world has no other magic. No wizards, no minor magicks, no
   enchanted objects beyond the crown and the oath-stones. If the
   prose introduces a "lesser charm" or a "village witch with
   small powers," that's a bug. The dragons were the magic.
   Without them, this is a cold mountain country with a smith and
   a sword.

7. Old-tongue mode in prose is triggered *only* by: (a) crown
   active, (b) dragon speaking, (c) Rowan dreaming post-bond.
   Never as decoration. Never for atmosphere. See voice.md
   Anti-Exemplar 5.
